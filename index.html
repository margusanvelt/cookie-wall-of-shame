The List What Counts Contribute

Under EU law, cookie consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous. Rejecting tracking cookies should be exactly as easy as accepting them. It almost never is.

This is a list of websites that use dark patterns in their cookie consent banners — making rejection deliberately harder, more confusing, or more time-consuming than acceptance. These practices violate the GDPR, the ePrivacy Directive, and guidance from the EDPB.

The CJEU ruled in Planet49 (C-673/17) that pre-ticked boxes do not constitute valid consent. The EDPB Cookie Banner Taskforce confirmed that asymmetric consent flows fail the "freely given" standard. These aren't edge cases. They're the law.

What Counts as a Dark Pattern?

We document cookie consent interfaces where the path to rejection is meaningfully harder than acceptance:

  • Hidden Reject — reject is buried behind extra screens or settings menus
  • Asymmetric Buttons — accept is a large coloured button; reject is a small text link
  • Pre-ticked Boxes — tracking categories enabled by default (illegal per Planet49)
  • Confusing Language — wording designed to mislead users into accepting
  • Forced Action — content blocked until cookies are accepted (cookie walls)
  • Nagging — repeated prompts to accept after the user has already declined
